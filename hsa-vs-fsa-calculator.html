Why the same tax bracket produces different results

Both accounts reduce your taxable income by the same rate. The difference isn't the tax math, it's what happens to money you don't spend. An HSA keeps it, indefinitely, even if you change jobs or health plans. An FSA is your employer's money again the moment the plan year ends, past whatever small rollover your plan allows.

When an FSA still wins

If you don't have a high-deductible health plan, you're not eligible for an HSA at all, and the FSA is your only pre-tax option. FSAs also allow full-year advance access to your full annual election on day one, which matters if you need a large expense covered early in the year before you've contributed that much yet. HSAs only hold what's actually been deposited so far.

What this tool doesn't account for

This estimates federal income tax, a flat state rate you enter, and FICA if contributed by payroll. It doesn't model itemized deductions, AMT, phase-outs at high income, or state-specific HSA tax treatment (a few states tax HSA contributions or earnings even though the federal government doesn't). Treat this as a planning estimate, not tax advice.
