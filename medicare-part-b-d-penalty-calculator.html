What will your Medicare late enrollment penalty cost you?

These penalties aren't one-time fees. Once assessed, they're added to your premium for as long as you're enrolled, which for most people means the rest of their life.

Part B

Penalty is 10% of the standard premium per each full 12-month period late.

Part D

Penalty is 1% of the national base beneficiary premium per month.

How this is calculated

Part B: for every full 12-month period you were eligible for Part B but didn't enroll, without qualifying employer coverage, add 10% of the standard premium, permanently. Part D: for every full month you went without creditable drug coverage after your initial enrollment window closed, add 1% of the national base beneficiary premium, rounded to the nearest ten cents, permanently.

When the penalty doesn't apply

If you or your spouse had qualifying coverage through current employment, generally a large enough employer plan, you can usually delay enrollment penalty-free and sign up during a Special Enrollment Period once that coverage ends. This calculator assumes you've already confirmed you don't qualify for that exception. If you're not sure, that's the first thing to check before treating this number as final.

Why the "lifetime" framing matters

A $20/month penalty sounds small. Over 20 years that's roughly $4,800, and Part B premiums also rise most years, so the real total is usually higher than a flat multiplication suggests. Treat the number above as a floor, not a ceiling.

What this doesn't account for

This doesn't model IRMAA income-related surcharges, which are separate from and stack on top of any late enrollment penalty. It also assumes the standard premium and base beneficiary premium stay flat across your selected years, when in practice they've increased most years historically. Confirm your specific situation with Medicare or a licensed advisor before making enrollment decisions based on this estimate.
